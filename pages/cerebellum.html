What Is the Cerebellum?

The cerebellum — Latin for "little brain" — sits at the back and bottom of the brain, tucked beneath the cerebral cortex. Despite making up only about 10% of the brain's total volume, it contains more than half of all the neurons in your entire brain. That density hints at just how much processing work it quietly handles in the background.

Unlike the cerebral cortex which makes conscious decisions, the cerebellum works automatically and below the level of awareness. It receives information from your muscles, joints, and balance organs, then fine-tunes your movements in real time to make them smooth, accurate, and coordinated.

More Neurons Than the Rest of the Brain Combined

One of the most remarkable facts about the cerebellum is its neuron density. It contains around 69 billion neurons — mostly a type called granule cells — compared to roughly 16 billion in the cerebral cortex. These neurons are packed into a tightly folded structure that, if unfolded, would stretch out to roughly the size of a full sheet of paper.

This massive neural network allows the cerebellum to make thousands of tiny real-time corrections every second. When you reach for a glass of water, it is the cerebellum — not your conscious mind — that makes sure your hand moves at exactly the right speed and stops precisely when it should.

Did You Know

In 1996, a woman was discovered to have been living her entire life without a cerebellum — a condition called cerebellar agenesis. She had no idea until she was 24 years old. She could walk and talk, though with difficulty. Her case showed how remarkably the rest of the brain can compensate, and raised major questions about everything scientists thought they knew about the cerebellum.

What Happens When It's Damaged?

Damage to the cerebellum does not cause paralysis, but it severely disrupts coordination:

  • Ataxia — wide, unsteady, lurching gait when walking
  • Tremors that appear during intentional movement
  • Slurred and irregular speech
  • Difficulty with fine motor tasks like writing or buttoning a shirt
  • Abnormal eye movements and dizziness
  • Problems with balance even when standing still
