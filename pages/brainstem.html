What Is the Brainstem?

The brainstem is the oldest and most essential part of the brain. It sits at the base of the brain, connecting the cerebral cortex above to the spinal cord below, acting as the main highway for signals traveling between the brain and the rest of the body. Without the brainstem, none of the higher brain functions would matter because the body simply could not survive.

It is made up of three sections stacked on top of each other: the midbrain at the top, the pons in the middle, and the medulla oblongata at the bottom. Each section handles a different set of critical life-sustaining functions that run entirely on autopilot, 24 hours a day, every day of your life.

Key Functions

💓

Heart Rate

The medulla regulates your heartbeat and blood pressure automatically.

🫁

Breathing

Controls the rhythm of breathing even while you are completely unconscious.

😴

Sleep & Wake

The reticular formation manages your sleep-wake cycle and alertness levels.

🤢

Reflexes

Controls automatic reflexes like swallowing, coughing, sneezing, and vomiting.

👁️

Eye Movement

The midbrain controls pupil dilation and basic eye movement reflexes.

🔀

Signal Relay

Routes all motor and sensory signals between the brain and spinal cord.

The Three Sections

The midbrain is the smallest and uppermost section. It handles visual and auditory reflexes — like automatically turning your head toward a sudden loud sound — and plays a key role in motor control and dopamine production through a region called the substantia nigra. Degeneration of this area is what causes Parkinson's disease.

The pons sits in the middle and acts as a bridge connecting the cerebellum to the rest of the brain. It regulates sleep, controls facial movements and sensations, and plays a key role in the transitions between the stages of sleep — particularly REM sleep, where dreaming occurs.

The medulla oblongata is the lowest section and arguably the most critical for survival. It directly controls heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing. Even the slightest damage to the medulla can be immediately fatal.

Did You Know

Brain death is defined medically as the complete and irreversible loss of all brainstem function. When the brainstem stops working, breathing and heartbeat cannot be sustained without machines — even if the rest of the brain shows some activity. This is why the brainstem is considered the absolute core of life itself.

What Happens When It's Damaged?

Brainstem damage is among the most serious injuries possible:

  • Loss of breathing and need for mechanical ventilation
  • Locked-in syndrome — full consciousness but complete paralysis
  • Coma or persistent vegetative state
  • Loss of ability to swallow, speak, or control facial muscles
  • Irregular heart rate and blood pressure instability
  • Complete brain death if the medulla is severely damaged
