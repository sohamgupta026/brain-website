What Is the Hippocampus?

The hippocampus is a small curved structure buried deep inside the temporal lobe, one on each side of the brain. Its name comes from the Greek word for seahorse because of its distinctive curved shape. Despite its small size, it is one of the most studied and important structures in the entire brain — because without it, you cannot form any new long-term memories.

It sits at the heart of the brain's limbic system, the network of structures responsible for emotion, motivation, and memory. The hippocampus acts as a gateway — deciding which experiences are important enough to be converted from short-term impressions into permanent long-term memories stored across the cortex.

Key Functions

💾

Memory Formation

Converts short-term experiences into long-term memories stored in the cortex.

🗺️

Spatial Navigation

Contains place cells that map your environment and track your location.

📅

Episodic Memory

Stores memories of personal life events with context of time and place.

🔗

Memory Consolidation

During sleep, replays and strengthens memories recorded during the day.

😰

Stress Response

Regulates the stress hormone cortisol and helps manage emotional memories.

🔄

Pattern Separation

Distinguishes between similar memories so they don't get confused with each other.

The Case of Patient H.M.

The most famous case in neuroscience history belongs to a man known only as H.M. In 1953, surgeon William Beecher Scoville removed most of H.M.'s hippocampus on both sides of his brain in an attempt to cure his severe epilepsy. The surgery worked — but the consequences were devastating and unexpected.

From that moment on, H.M. was completely unable to form any new long-term memories. Every person he met, every conversation he had, every experience he lived — all of it vanished within minutes. He could remember everything from before the surgery perfectly, but his life after it was an endless present with no ability to build a future. He lived this way for 55 years until his death in 2008, and his case transformed our entire understanding of how memory works.

Did You Know

London taxi drivers have a significantly larger hippocampus than average. A famous study found that the more years a driver had spent navigating London's enormously complex street network, the larger their hippocampus had grown. It was one of the first clear pieces of evidence that the adult human brain can physically change and grow in response to experience — a property called neuroplasticity.

The Hippocampus and Alzheimer's Disease

The hippocampus is one of the first regions of the brain to be damaged by Alzheimer's disease, which is why memory loss — especially of recent events — is the earliest and most recognizable symptom. As the disease progresses and spreads beyond the hippocampus into the cortex, patients gradually lose older memories, language, and eventually all cognitive function.

What Happens When It's Damaged?

  • Anterograde amnesia — inability to form any new long-term memories
  • Severe disorientation and inability to navigate familiar environments
  • Loss of episodic memory — personal life events and experiences
  • Difficulty distinguishing between similar memories or situations
  • Increased anxiety and difficulty regulating stress responses
  • Early-stage Alzheimer's symptoms including forgetfulness and confusion
